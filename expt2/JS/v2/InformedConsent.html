The purpose of this study is to investigate how the demands on attention (like task difficulty) affect memory.

Procedure: In this experiment, you will be presented with visual images (e.g., images of fractals, faces, houses, words, as well as Arabic numerals), which will require you to press a key depending on the current task instructions. For instance, you may be asked to detect an upside-down face image via a button response. Once you are finished with the task, you will also be asked to fill out a few questionnaires about your attitudes and state of mind during the experiment. If any item on the questionnaires makes you uncomfortable, feel free to skip it, but please try to answer as many of the questions as you can. Based on our piloting, this task should take about 70 minutes to complete.

Participation: Participation in this study is voluntary. You may decline to participate at any time without penalty to you. If you decide to participate, you may withdraw from the study at any time by closing your Internet browser window.

Risks and Benefits: We know of no risks or benefits to you for participating in this research study. The potential benefits of this research lie in the knowledge that will be acquired.

Confidentiality: This research is confidential. Amazon Mechanical Turk will provide us, the researchers, only with your responses. We will not have access to your personal information.

Compensation: For participating in this study, you will earn $7.50 upon completion. We will screen your responses prior to compensation. If you do not complete the task, or we think that you completed it to an unsatisfactory standard (i.e. you do not follow the instructions), you will not be compensated.

Contact Information: If you have additional questions regarding this study, please feel free to ask by emailing the researcher. If questions arise after the experiment, you may contact Professor Tobias Egner at tobias.egner@duke.edu or 919-684-1034 or the Human Subjects Protection at campusirb@duke.edu regarding your rights as a research subject.
